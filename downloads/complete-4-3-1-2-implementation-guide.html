Introduction

How to Get the Most From This Guide

A practical tactical guide for grassroots and amateur soccer coaches

The 4-3-1-2 is a narrow, aggressive, central formation built around midfield control, a creative number 10, and two strikers. It gives the team:

  • A back four
  • Three central midfielders (one holding, two number 8s)
  • One number 10
  • Two strikers
  • Strong central overloads
  • A clear front-two partnership
  • Good support around the ball
  • Strong counter-pressing potential
  • A natural route to combine through the middle
  • A clear identity for teams with strong central players

The 4-3-1-2 is often called a diamond midfield. It is one of the best systems for teams that have good central midfielders but lack natural wingers. But it has a clear weakness: no natural wide midfielders or wingers. The fullbacks must provide width when attacking, and the central midfielders must help protect wide areas when defending. This formation must be coached carefully.

Section 1

Formation Overview

What it is, when to use it, and what to watch out for

The 4-3-1-2 is a formation built around central overloads and close support. The three central midfielders form the base — usually one holding midfielder and two number 8s. The number 10 plays ahead of them. The two strikers lead the line. This gives strong central connections. The shape is narrow by nature: strong because it overloads central areas, risky because it can leave wide spaces open.

Best For

  • Two reliable strikers
  • A creative number 10
  • Strong central midfielders
  • A disciplined holding midfielder
  • Fullbacks with good fitness and timing
  • A team that wants to play through central areas
  • A team without natural wingers
  • A team that wants strong counter-pressing access
  • Players who can combine in tight spaces
  • A front two that can link, run, and press together

Avoid If

  • You rely heavily on natural wingers
  • Your fullbacks cannot provide width
  • Your midfielders do not shift wide defensively
  • Your number 10 does not work out of possession
  • Your strikers do not press
  • Your team struggles defending switches of play
  • Your central players cannot play under pressure
  • Your team regularly gets exposed in wide areas
  • Your fullbacks lack the fitness to attack and recover
Main Strengths
  • Strong central overloads
  • A clear number 10 role
  • Two strikers as a partnership
  • Good support around the ball
  • Strong counter-pressing potential
  • A compact defensive structure
  • Good central combinations
  • Strong second-ball presence
  • Ability to play through midfield
  • Clear identity for teams with strong central players
Main Risks
  • Lack of natural width
  • Fullbacks get overloaded
  • Opponents switch into wide spaces
  • Both fullbacks attack together
  • Midfielders get dragged wide
  • Number 10 becomes a luxury player
  • Strikers disconnect from midfield
  • Team becomes too narrow in attack
  • Wide areas exposed in transition
  • Teams with strong wingers cause problems
Section 2

Default Shape

Diamond structure · unit relationships · key partnerships · critical distances

The default 4-3-1-2: 1 GK · 2 center backs · 2 fullbacks · 1 holding midfielder · 2 number 8s · 1 number 10 · 2 strikers. The number 10 connects midfield to the strikers. The number 8s provide energy and support. The holding midfielder protects the back four. The fullbacks provide width. The strikers give depth, link play, and central threat. A strong 4-3-1-2 is compact through the middle and smart with its width. A poor 4-3-1-2 is narrow, predictable, and exposed wide.

Unit Relationships

Defensive Unit

GK + back four. Defend the goal, build attacks, and provide width at the right time. Fullbacks are the primary source of width — this is their most important attacking responsibility.

Midfield Three

Hold CM + two No.8s. Control central areas, support possession, protect the defense, and shift wide when needed. The No.8s must be ready to support both attack and defense.

Number 10

Links midfield and front two. Create chances, support strikers, press intelligently, and block central passes. If the No.10 does not work defensively, the midfield three are exposed.

Front Two

Two strikers. Provide depth, combine constantly, run channels, press from the front, and finish attacks. They are the team's most important partnership — different movements every time.

Key Partnerships

Fullback ↔ Number 8

The most important defensive relationship. When the fullback goes forward, the number 8 may protect the wide channel. When the number 8 moves wide, the fullback may stay deeper. They cannot both leave the space at the same time.

Holding CM ↔ Back Four

Holding CM protects the space in front of the center backs. Must stay central and stop counterattacks. Never gets dragged wide. This player is the defensive anchor of the diamond.

Number 10 ↔ Strikers

No.10 must stay close enough to connect with the front two. Too deep → strikers become isolated. Too high and not defending → midfield gets exposed. The No.10 must manage both constantly.

Striker Partnership

Two strikers must make different movements every time: one short, one behind · one central, one channel · one near post, one far post. If both do the same thing, the number 10 has no options and the midfield has no outlet.

Default Shape Coaching Points

  • Midfield three protect the middle
  • Number 10 connects to the front two
  • Fullbacks provide width at the right time
  • Number 8s support both central and wide areas
  • Strikers work as a pair — different movements
  • Holding midfielder stays disciplined
  • Team must not become too narrow in attack
  • If the press is broken, recover into a compact block

Attacking Shape

From 4-3-1-2 to 3-2-5 — creating width without wingers

The 4-3-1-2 can attack as a 3-2-5, but it needs clear rotations. Because there are no wingers, width must be created through fullbacks, strikers drifting into channels, or number 8s moving wider at the right time. The simple rule: one fullback goes, one fullback balances.

Fullback Attacking Triggers

✓ Fullback Can Attack When
  • Possession is secure
  • Ball controlled by midfield or CBs
  • Opposite fullback is balancing
  • Holding CM is protecting
  • Ball-side No.8 can support or cover
  • Opponent is forced back
⚠ Fullback Be Cautious When
  • Ball is loose
  • Opponent has fast wide players
  • Team is stretched
  • Holding CM is exposed
  • Opposite fullback is already high
  • Press has just been broken

Role of the Number 10 in Attack

The number 10 is the key attacking player. They should find space between the lines, combine with strikers, play forward passes, receive on the half-turn, and attack the edge of the box. The number 10 must not become disconnected — close enough to the strikers and available enough for the midfield at all times.

Role of the Strikers in Attack

Striker Movement Combinations

One short, one long · One central, one channel · One near post, one far post · One linking play, one running beyond · One drifting wide, one staying central. The strikers must not both make the same movement. This creates space for the number 10 and midfield runners — and gives the team a clear route forward from every situation.

Rest Defense

Rest defense is vital in the 4-3-1-2 — the formation can leave wide spaces open if the team attacks without balance. Simple rules: if one fullback goes, the other balances · if one number 8 joins, the other connects · the holding midfielder stays central · if the ball is lost, stop the first forward pass.

Attacking Coaching Points

  • Are we too narrow?
  • Is one fullback providing width?
  • Is the opposite fullback balancing?
  • Is the number 10 finding space between the lines?
  • Are the two strikers working as a pair — different movements?
  • Are number 8s supporting at the right time?
  • Is the holding midfielder protecting underneath?
  • Are we creating five attacking threats?
  • Are we ready to counter-press if possession is lost?
Section 4

Defensive Shape

Compact 4-3-1-2 block and 4-4-2 mid-block principles for wide protection

The natural defensive shape is a compact 4-3-1-2 — very good at stopping teams from playing through the middle. The problem is wide areas. The team must shift quickly and protect the fullbacks. When the game becomes stretched, the team defends with 4-4-2 mid-block principles.

🛡️
The Central Strength
The 4-3-1-2 clogs central areas — hard to play through central midfield, through the No.10 space, into the strikers' feet, or through central passing lanes. Good counter-pressing access because players are close together. When you lose it centrally, several players can react immediately.

4-4-2 Mid-Block Principles (Fallback Shape)

The 4-3-1-2 cannot always become a perfect flat 4-4-2, but it can defend with 4-4-2 principles when needed:

  • Front line screens central passes
  • Number 10 supports the press or joins a striker
  • Ball-side number 8 shifts wide
  • Far-side number 8 tucks in
  • Holding midfielder protects the middle
  • Back four stay compact
  • Fullbacks do not jump without cover

Key message: Protect the middle first, then shift wide together.

Defensive Priorities

  1. Protect the Middle — this is the main strength; do not allow easy central passes
  2. Shift Wide Together — when the ball goes wide, the whole team travels as a unit
  3. Do Not Let Fullbacks Defend Alone — fullbacks can be overloaded; number 8s must help
  4. Press on Triggers — do not chase constantly; press when the moment is right
  5. Recover if the Press Is Broken — if the opponent escapes pressure, recover into the compact block
Front Two Responsibilities
  • Screen passes into central midfield
  • Force play one way
  • Press CBs on triggers
  • Stop easy switches
  • Work as a pair · be ready to counterattack
Number 10 Responsibilities
  • Screen central passes
  • Help the front two press
  • Block the opposition holding midfielder
  • Recover quickly when bypassed
  • Support counter-pressing

⚠ If No.10 does not work, midfield three are exposed.

Midfield Three Responsibilities
  • Screen central passes · shift wide when needed
  • Track runners · win second balls
  • Protect fullbacks · protect back four
  • Holding CM stays central
  • No.8s ready to help wide areas
Pressing Triggers
  • Poor touch · ball backwards
  • Ball into central midfield under pressure
  • Ball played wide with slow speed
  • Fullback near sideline · opponent facing own goal
  • Slow or under-hit pass

Defensive Coaching Points

  • Are we protecting the middle?
  • Are fullbacks getting isolated?
  • Are number 8s shifting wide quickly enough?
  • Is the number 10 working out of possession?
  • Are the strikers screening central passes?
  • Are we forcing play into predictable areas?
  • Are we pressing together or individually?
  • Are we recovering when the press is broken?
  • Are we too narrow when the ball is switched?
Section 5

Transitions

One of the biggest strengths of the 4-3-1-2 — and the wide danger that must be managed

Transitions are one of the biggest strengths of the 4-3-1-2. Because the team is compact and central, players are often close to the ball when possession changes. This gives the team strong counter-pressing access. But the wide transition danger must be managed — if the ball is lost when a fullback is high, the team must react quickly.

Defensive Transition — When We Lose the Ball

⚡
The Counter-Press Rule
Counter-press for 6–8 seconds. This formation is built for it — central players are close together. Aim: stop the first forward pass, force the opponent backwards, delay the counterattack, and allow fullbacks to recover.

Defensive Transition Priorities

  1. Nearest player presses — immediate pressure
  2. Second player covers — blocks the next pass
  3. Holding CM protects the middle — must not get dragged away
  4. Number 8s protect wide channels — if a fullback is high, the nearest No.8 covers
  5. Recover if the ball escapes — recover quickly into the compact block
The 6–8 Second Rule

Press if: ball is loose · poor touch · players are close · opponent facing own goal · passing options limited · ball is central and can be surrounded.

Recover if: opponent has control · ball has been switched wide · fullback is caught high · holding CM is exposed · opponent can easily play forward. Intensity without chaos.

Attacking Transition — When We Win the Ball

The 4-3-1-2 can be very dangerous after winning the ball because the number 10 and two strikers are already central — the opponent may be open through the middle. Forward options: striker into feet · striker running behind · number 10 between the lines · second striker in the channel · fullback wide if safe · number 8 supporting underneath.

Attacking Transition Priorities

  • First look forward
  • Number 10 finds space between the lines
  • Strikers split or combine
  • One striker can run into a channel
  • One number 8 supports underneath
  • Fullback runs if the wide space is open
  • Holding CM protects balance
  • Secure possession if forward is not on

Transition Coaching Points

  • Do we react immediately when possession is lost?
  • Are we close enough to counter-press?
  • Are we stopping the opponent's first forward pass?
  • Is the holding CM protecting the middle?
  • Are number 8s protecting wide channels?
  • When we win it, do we look into the number 10 or strikers?
  • Are strikers making different movements?
  • Are we attacking with purpose or forcing the ball?
Section 6

Key Player Roles

All eight positions across all phases of play

🥅 Goalkeeper GK

In Possession

  • Support build-up · play into CBs or fullbacks
  • Find the holding CM · play longer into strikers when needed
  • Help switch play · stay calm under pressure

Out of Possession

  • Organize the back four · communicate with holding CM
  • Manage space behind the defense
  • Command the box · defend crosses
🛡️ Center Backs CB

In Possession

  • Split to receive · play into holding CM
  • Find fullbacks · switch play
  • Carry forward when space opens

Out of Possession

  • Stay compact · defend central spaces
  • Track runners · control the defensive line
  • Defend crosses · cover when fullbacks attack
🏃 Fullbacks FB

In Possession

  • Provide width when possession is secure
  • Overlap or underlap · deliver crosses
  • Switch play · balance when opposite FB attacks

Out of Possession

  • Defend wide areas · stop crosses
  • Stay connected to CBs · communicate with No.8s
  • Tuck in far side · recover quickly after attacking

⚠ Never both attack at the same time.

⚙️ Holding Midfielder DM

In Possession

  • Receive from defenders · support underneath
  • Switch play · control tempo · offer safe pass
  • Connect with No.8s and No.10

Out of Possession

  • Screen central passes · protect back four
  • Stop counterattacks · track runners
  • Stay central — never get dragged wide

"Your job is to protect the middle. Do not get dragged away from the danger zone."

⚡ Number 8s CM

In Possession

  • Support build-up · connect with fullbacks
  • Support the No.10 · make forward runs
  • Help create width · arrive around the box

Out of Possession

  • Protect central areas · shift wide to help fullbacks
  • Track runners · press opposition midfielders
  • Support the holding CM
🎨 Number 10 AM

In Possession

  • Find space between the lines
  • Combine with strikers · receive on the half-turn
  • Play forward passes · attack the edge of the box
  • Support counterattacks

Out of Possession

  • Screen central passes · help front two press
  • Block opposition holding CM
  • Recover when bypassed · support counter-press
🎯 Strikers ST

In Possession

  • Combine with each other — different movements
  • Run channels · provide depth
  • Hold the ball · attack crosses
  • Link with the number 10

Out of Possession

  • Press together · screen central passes
  • Force play one way
  • Stop easy switches
  • Delay the opposition build-up
  • Be ready to counterattack

⚠ Must make different movements — not the same run at the same time. If both come short, no depth. If both run behind, no link play.

Section 7

Training Routine

Five sessions — each one connected directly to match day

The aim is to build central control, front-two connection, number 10 influence, fullback width, and transition discipline. Training must connect directly to match day.

Session 1
Default Shape and Central Connections
Objective

Help players understand the 4-3-1-2 structure — especially the midfield diamond, number 10, and front-two partnership.

Setup

Half a field or two-thirds. Organize in 4-3-1-2, start unopposed then add passive opposition. Walk through: back four · holding CM · No.8 positions · No.10 · striker partnership · fullback width · defensive shifting.

Practice — Ball-Movement Triggers
  • Ball with CB · Ball with fullback · Ball with holding CM
  • Ball with No.8 · Ball with No.10 · Ball lost · Ball won
Coaching Points
  • Stay connected centrally · No.10 links midfield to attack
  • Strikers work as a pair — different movements
  • Fullbacks provide width at the right time · holding CM protects middle
  • No.8s support and shift wide · do not become too narrow
Progression

Add opposition pressure. Build through central combinations, use fullbacks for width, and recover quickly when possession is lost.

Match-Day Link

This session gives the team its base structure and relationships.

Session 2
Attacking Through the Diamond
Objective

Teach the team how to attack through the midfield diamond and front two.

Setup

Two-thirds of field. Team in 4-3-1-2. Opposition: back four and midfield screen.

Practice Rules
  • No.10 must receive between the lines · strikers make different movements
  • One fullback provides width · opposite fullback balances
  • One No.8 can support higher · holding CM protects underneath
Coaching Points
  • Can we find the No.10? · Are strikers making different movements?
  • Are fullbacks providing width? · Is the holding CM protecting?
  • Are we creating five attacking threats? · Are we too narrow?
Progression

Add a counterattack goal for the opposition if the attacking team loses possession — forces rest defense thinking.

Match-Day Link

This session teaches the team to attack centrally without becoming exposed wide.

Session 3
Defending and Protecting Wide Areas
Objective

Teach the team how to defend compactly while preventing fullbacks from being isolated — the biggest defensive problem in the 4-3-1-2.

Setup

Half to three-quarters of field. Defending team in 4-3-1-2. Opposition builds through central and wide areas.

Coaching Points
  • Strikers screen central passes · No.10 helps block midfield
  • Holding CM protects center · No.8s shift to help fullbacks
  • Back four stay connected · do not leave fullbacks 1v2
  • Press on clear triggers only
Pressing Triggers
  • Poor touch · ball backwards · ball played slowly into wide areas
  • Fullback near sideline · opponent facing own goal · slow/under-hit pass
Progression

Allow the defending team to counterattack through the No.10 and front two after winning possession.

Match-Day Link

This session solves the biggest defensive issue in the 4-3-1-2: wide protection.

Session 4
Transitions and Counter-Pressing
Objective

Train the team's reaction when possession changes — central counter-pressing and protecting wide spaces.

Setup

7v7, 8v8, or 9v9. Create lots of turnovers. Use wide channels if possible to expose fullback spaces.

Coaching Points — Defensive Transition
  • Nearest player presses · second player covers
  • Holding CM protects middle · No.8s protect wide channels
  • Fullbacks recover · stop first forward pass
Coaching Points — Attacking Transition
  • First look forward · No.10 finds space
  • Strikers split or combine · one No.8 supports
  • Holding CM protects · secure possession if forward is not on
Progression — Bonus Points For:
  • Winning ball back within 8 seconds
  • Finding the No.10 after regaining possession
  • Creating a chance through the front two
  • Recovering into shape after losing possession
  • Preventing a counterattack into the wide channel

This session builds the central intensity and transition discipline that makes the 4-3-1-2 effective.

Section 8

Match-Day Checklist

Before the game · During the game · Half-time · After the game

Before the Game — Ask Yourself

  • Do players know the default 4-3-1-2 shape?
  • Does the number 10 understand their full role — attacking and defensive?
  • Do strikers understand their partnership and movement rules?
  • Do fullbacks know when to attack and when to stay?
  • Do number 8s know how to protect wide areas?
  • Does the holding midfielder know to stay central?
  • Do players know the counter-pressing rule?
  • Do players know how to attack as a 3-2-5?
  • Have I kept the message simple enough?

Team Talk — Keep It Simple

Example Team Talk
"We start in a 4-3-1-2 because it gives us strength through the middle, a number 10 to connect play, and two strikers to threaten the goal. When we attack, we need fullback width and support around the front two. When we lose it, we react for 6–8 seconds. If we cannot win it back, we recover into a compact block and protect the middle first."

Players need clarity.

During the Game — Watch the Team, Not Just the Ball

  • Are we too narrow?
  • Is one fullback providing width?
  • Is the opposite fullback balancing?
  • Are fullbacks being exposed?
  • Are number 8s helping wide areas?
  • Is the number 10 involved and connected?
  • Are the strikers making different movements?
  • Is the holding CM protecting the middle?
  • Are we counter-pressing together?
  • Are we recovering if the press is broken?

Half-Time — Use a Simple Structure

1. What Is Working?
"We are finding the number 10 well and combining through the middle."
2. What Needs Fixing?
"When we lose the ball, our fullbacks are exposed because our number 8s are too slow to protect the wide channel."
3. The Clear Instruction
"When one fullback goes, the nearest number 8 must be ready to cover the channel, and the holding midfielder must stay central."
4. The Belief Message
"The central control is working. Keep connecting through the number 10, but protect the wide spaces quicker."

After the Game — Review

  • Did the 4-3-1-2 suit the team?
  • Did the number 10 influence the game?
  • Did the front two work as a partnership?
  • Did fullbacks provide enough width?
  • Were fullbacks exposed?
  • Did number 8s protect wide areas?
  • Did the defensive shape protect us?
  • Did transitions work?
  • What problem repeated?
  • What should we train next?
The Review Rule

Turn the biggest problem into next week's training topic.

Section 9

Common Problems and Fixes

Seven recurring problems with practical solutions

1The Team Is Too Narrow
Why It Happens
  • No natural wingers · fullbacks stay too deep · strikers and No.10 all stay central · No.8s do not support wide areas · opponent blocks the middle
Fix
  • Allow one fullback to provide width · use switches of play · ask one striker to drift into the channel · ask No.8s to support wider when needed · stretch the opponent before attacking inside
"Our strength is central, but we still need width. One fullback must stretch the pitch when we have control."
2Fullbacks Get Exposed
Why It Happens
  • Fullbacks attack without cover · No.8s do not shift wide · opponent switches play quickly · holding CM gets dragged out · both fullbacks attack together
Fix
  • One fullback attacks, one balances · nearest No.8 covers wide channel · holding CM stays central · far-side fullback tucks in · counter-press immediately after losing the ball
"Fullbacks give us width, but they cannot be left alone. Number 8s must protect the channel."
3The Number 10 Becomes a Luxury Player
Why It Happens
  • Number 10 only wants the ball · does not press or screen · does not recover when bypassed · leaves midfield three exposed
Fix
  • Give clear defensive jobs · screen opposition holding CM · join the press when triggered · recover when the ball goes past · stay connected to the midfield
"Number 10, you are our creative link, but you are also part of our defensive structure."
4The Strikers Make the Same Runs
Why It Happens
  • No clear partnership rules · both come short · both run behind · both attack the same area · No.10 has no clear passing options
Fix
  • One short, one long · one central, one channel · one near post, one far post · one links, one threatens depth · use the No.10 to connect them
"Front two, do not make the same run. One shows, one stretches."
5The Holding Midfielder Gets Dragged Around
Why It Happens
  • Chases wide · No.8s do not recover · fullbacks need help · opponent pulls the team out of shape
Fix
  • Holding CM stays central · No.8s help wide areas · CBs communicate · protect the middle first · do not chase into areas that leave the center open
"Holding midfielder, your job is to protect the middle. Do not get dragged away from the danger zone."
6The Team Cannot Break Wide Pressure
Why It Happens
  • Fullback receives with no support · No.8 too far away · no winger to help · striker does not run channel · opponent traps wide areas
Fix
  • No.8 supports the fullback · striker runs into the channel · No.10 offers inside · use holding CM to switch play · opposite fullback prepares for the switch
"When the ball goes wide, we need three options: inside, back, and forward into the channel."
7The Team Gets Counterattacked Wide
Why It Happens
  • Fullbacks high · No.8s central · ball lost cheaply · counter-press is slow · opponent plays quickly into wide channels
Fix
  • Counter-press for 6–8 seconds · nearest No.8 protects the channel · opposite fullback balances · holding CM protects central space · recover into compact shape if press fails
"When we lose it, stop the first forward pass. If we cannot win it, recover and protect the wide channel."

Coach Scripts

Explaining the System

Our 4-3-1-2 Team System
Team Tactical Blueprint — Season Reference Card

⬛ Default Shape

We start in a 4-3-1-2:

  • Back four
  • Holding midfielder (anchor)
  • Two number 8s
  • Number 10
  • Two strikers
  • Strong central control

⚡ Attacking Shape (3-2-5)

When we have controlled possession:

  • Use the number 10 to connect play
  • Use the front two — different movements
  • One fullback provides width
  • Opposite fullback stays balanced
  • One No.8 can join the attack
  • Holding CM protects underneath

🛡️ Defensive Shape

Compact 4-3-1-2 and 4-4-2 mid-block principles:

  • Protect the middle first
  • Shift wide together
  • Do not leave fullbacks alone
  • No.8s help wide areas
  • Strikers screen and press
  • No.10 works defensively

↔ Transitions

Lose the ball: Counter-press 6–8 seconds. Cannot win it → recover into compact block, protect wide channels.

Win the ball: Look forward into No.10, strikers, or channels. If not on → secure first pass and build again.

My Role in the System

PositionKey Responsibility
GoalkeeperStart attacks, communicate, and organize the back four.
Center BacksStay connected, defend central spaces, and play into midfield when possible.
FullbacksProvide width in attack, defend wide areas, and balance when the opposite fullback attacks.
Holding MidfielderProtect the middle, support build-up, stop counterattacks, and never get dragged wide.
Number 8sSupport attacks, help fullbacks in wide areas, press with energy, and recover quickly.
Number 10Connect midfield to attack, create chances, support the strikers, and work defensively.
StrikersWork as a pair with different movements — link, run channels, finish attacks, and lead the press.

Our Non-Negotiables

  • Protect the middle first
  • Use the number 10 to connect play
  • Front two work together — never the same run
  • One fullback attacks, one always balances
  • Number 8s protect wide channels
  • Holding midfielder stays central — no exceptions
  • Counter-press if we are close enough
  • If we cannot press, recover into shape
  • Do not become too narrow in attack
  • The formation is the starting point — the team shape changes with the game

Final Coaching Message

The 4-3-1-2 is not successful just because it gives you central players. It works when the team understands how to use central overloads without becoming narrow and exposed.

The number 10 must connect the team. The front two must work as a partnership. The number 8s must support attacks and protect wide spaces. The holding midfielder must stay disciplined. The fullbacks must provide width at the right time. The team must attack with ambition, but defend with balance.

The 4-3-1-2 works when the players understand:

How we start
How we attack
How we defend
How the No.10 connects the team
How the front two work as a pair
How fullbacks provide width safely
How we react when we lose the ball
How we react when we win the ball

Start with the structure.
Build the fullback roles and timing.
Train the number 10 connections.
Develop the striker partnership.
Repeat the wide protection.
Review the game.
Improve one problem at a time.

That is how the 4-3-1-2 becomes more than a narrow system. That is how it becomes a compact, intelligent, dangerous team identity that dominates the middle and creates the right threats at the right time.
